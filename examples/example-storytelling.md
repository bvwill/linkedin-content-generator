```markdown
## Post

A PM I coach had 12 years of experience and couldn't get past the first round.

She'd done incredible work — launched products used by millions, led cross-functional teams of 20+, turned around a failing product line. Her resume was strong. Her stories were... not.

The problem wasn't what she'd accomplished. It was how she talked about it.

Every answer started with "we." We built this. We decided that. We shipped it.

After 45 minutes of coaching, I asked her one question: "What would have happened differently if you hadn't been on this project?"

She paused. Then she started describing specific decisions SHE made. The trade-off SHE pushed for when the team wanted to go a different direction. The data SHE pulled that changed the VP's mind.

Same story. Completely different impact.

Two weeks later she had two second-round interviews. A month after that, she had an offer.

The experience was always there. She just needed to stop hiding behind "we."

If your interview answers sound like team updates, you're leaving your best material on the table.

## Coaching Notes

- **Hook strength:** Strong — specific detail (12 years) + unexpected tension (can't get past first round) creates curiosity gap
- **Pillar alignment:** Perfect coaching stories fit — anonymized, pattern-based, emotionally resonant
- **Engagement potential:** High comment potential — many PMs will recognize the "we" problem in themselves
- **Alternative angles:** Could do a tips version ("3 ways to fix the 'we' problem in interviews"), could do a thought-leadership version about why PM culture encourages "we" language
```

---

## Why This Works

1. **Hook is immediate tension:** "12 years of experience" + "couldn't get past first round" — the reader has to know why
2. **"We" voice is absent:** The post itself models what it teaches — Will uses "I" and "she," never hiding behind generalities
3. **Specific coaching moment:** "What would have happened differently if you hadn't been on this project?" — this is a real technique, not a platitude
4. **Resolution is concrete:** Two second rounds, one offer. Not "she felt more confident."
5. **Takeaway is actionable:** The reader can immediately audit their own interview answers
6. **No sell:** This is a coaching story, not a pitch. Trust is built through demonstration.
